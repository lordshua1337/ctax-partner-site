Tax Resolution Glossary

Plain-language definitions of IRS tax resolution terms used by CPAs, tax preparers, and financial professionals.

Offer in Compromise (OIC)
An IRS program that allows taxpayers to settle their tax debt for less than the full amount owed. The IRS evaluates the taxpayer's income, assets, expenses, and ability to pay to determine an acceptable offer amount. Not all taxpayers qualify — the IRS acceptance rate for OICs is approximately 30-40% of submitted applications. The process typically takes 6-18 months from submission to decision.
Installment Agreement
A payment plan between a taxpayer and the IRS that allows the taxpayer to pay their tax debt in monthly installments over time, rather than in a single lump sum. Installment agreements can be set up for balances of any size, though terms vary. Short-term agreements (120 days or less) and long-term agreements (up to 72 months) are available depending on the amount owed and the taxpayer's financial situation.
Currently Not Collectible (CNC)
A status the IRS assigns to a taxpayer's account when they demonstrate they cannot afford to pay their tax debt without causing financial hardship. While in CNC status, the IRS temporarily suspends collection activity — no levies, garnishments, or seizures. The debt remains on the books and accrues interest and penalties, but the taxpayer is not required to make payments. The IRS reviews CNC status periodically.
Penalty Abatement
The reduction or removal of IRS penalties assessed on a tax account. The IRS may abate penalties for reasonable cause (such as serious illness, natural disaster, or reliance on professional advice), first-time penalty abatement (for taxpayers with a clean compliance history), or statutory exceptions. Penalty abatement does not reduce the underlying tax owed — it only removes the penalty charges, which can amount to 25% or more of the original tax liability.
Innocent Spouse Relief
An IRS provision that relieves a taxpayer from liability for tax debt resulting from their spouse's (or former spouse's) errors or omissions on a jointly filed tax return. To qualify, the requesting spouse must demonstrate they did not know about and had no reason to know about the understatement of tax. Innocent Spouse Relief is filed using IRS Form 8857 and applies only to jointly filed returns.
Tax Lien
A legal claim the IRS places against a taxpayer's property — including real estate, personal property, and financial assets — when the taxpayer fails to pay a tax debt after receiving a notice and demand for payment. A federal tax lien affects the taxpayer's credit and attaches to all current and future assets. The lien is released within 30 days of paying the debt in full or when the IRS accepts an Offer in Compromise.
Tax Levy
The legal seizure of a taxpayer's property or assets to satisfy an unpaid tax debt. Unlike a lien (which is a claim against property), a levy is the actual taking of property. The IRS can levy bank accounts, wages, Social Security benefits, retirement accounts, and other income or assets. The IRS must provide a Final Notice of Intent to Levy at least 30 days before seizing assets, giving the taxpayer time to respond or arrange payment.
Wage Garnishment
A type of levy in which the IRS directs a taxpayer's employer to withhold a portion of their wages and send it directly to the IRS to pay outstanding tax debt. IRS wage garnishments are more aggressive than most creditor garnishments — the IRS determines the exempt amount (the portion the taxpayer keeps) based on filing status and number of dependents using Publication 1494. The remaining wages are sent to the IRS until the debt is paid or an alternative arrangement is made.
IRS Form 2848 (Power of Attorney)
The IRS form used to authorize a tax professional — such as an Enrolled Agent, CPA, or attorney — to represent a taxpayer before the IRS. Form 2848 grants the authorized representative the ability to receive confidential tax information, negotiate on behalf of the taxpayer, sign agreements, and attend IRS meetings. The authorization is specific to the tax matters and periods listed on the form.
Revenue Officer
An IRS employee assigned to collect unpaid taxes from taxpayers with significant outstanding balances. Revenue Officers are field agents who visit taxpayers in person, unlike IRS phone agents. They have the authority to seize assets, file liens, levy bank accounts, and pursue other collection actions. Cases assigned to Revenue Officers are typically higher-dollar and higher-priority, and having professional representation when dealing with a Revenue Officer is strongly recommended.
Tax Investigation
The first phase of the Community Tax resolution process. During the Tax Investigation, a tax professional reviews the client's complete IRS account — including all tax years with balances, penalties, interest, and compliance status — to determine which resolution programs the client qualifies for. The investigation produces a clear picture of the client's options, expected timelines, and costs before they commit to resolution services. The Community Tax investigation fee is $295 for individuals.
Enrolled Agent (EA)
A federally authorized tax practitioner licensed by the IRS with unlimited rights to represent taxpayers before the IRS. Enrolled Agents must pass the Special Enrollment Examination (a comprehensive three-part exam) or have qualifying experience as a former IRS employee. Unlike CPAs or attorneys, Enrolled Agents specialize exclusively in taxation and are authorized to represent taxpayers in all 50 states.
Collection Due Process (CDP) Hearing
A formal hearing a taxpayer can request after receiving a Notice of Intent to Levy or a Notice of Federal Tax Lien Filing. The CDP hearing is conducted by the IRS Office of Appeals and gives the taxpayer the opportunity to challenge the collection action, propose alternative payment arrangements, or raise other issues. The request must be filed within 30 days of the notice date. During the CDP process, collection activity is generally suspended.
IRS Fresh Start Program
A set of IRS policy changes introduced in 2011 and expanded in subsequent years to make it easier for taxpayers to resolve their tax debts. Fresh Start includes expanded access to Installment Agreements (raising the threshold for streamlined agreements to $50,000), more favorable Offer in Compromise calculations, and changes to lien filing thresholds. The Fresh Start Program is not a single program taxpayers apply to — it refers to the updated guidelines that apply to all taxpayers seeking resolution.
Tax Gap
The difference between the total amount of taxes owed to the IRS and the amount actually collected. According to IRS estimates, the gross tax gap exceeds $600 billion annually. The tax gap represents unpaid taxes from non-filing, underreporting, and underpayment. A significant portion of the tax gap consists of individual taxpayers with assessed balances who have not paid — the population most likely to need tax resolution services.
Statute of Limitations on Collection (CSED)
The IRS generally has 10 years from the date a tax is assessed to collect the outstanding balance. This is known as the Collection Statute Expiration Date (CSED). After the CSED passes, the IRS can no longer legally collect the debt and it is removed from the taxpayer's account. Certain actions — such as filing an Offer in Compromise, requesting a Collection Due Process hearing, or filing for bankruptcy — can pause or extend the 10-year collection period.
